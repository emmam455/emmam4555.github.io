Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that, as you can see in this video, is characterized by essential tremors of the extremities (like the hands). It is a progressive disorder in which one gradually develops symptoms including the aforementioned tremors, bradykinesia or slowed movement, rigid muscles, impaired posture, and speech challenges and difficulties Many patients also have struggles with mental health and sleeping due to the damage caused to their brain in the course of the disease. In early stages, many patients also have plain affects, displaying little emotion in their face. Patients usually develop Parkinson's disease later in life, and the disease disproportionately affects males over females. It is also a hereditary disease, meaning that individuals with family members diagnosed with disease are at a higher risk of developing it later in life. Similarly to ALS, this is a disease that currently has no cure, but for which many are in development. There are some treatments that can help with symptom management, which I will expand upon in the next paragraph.

The molecular hallmark of Parkinson's disease is the formation of protein aggregates known as Lewy bodies, specifically in the substantia nigra of the brain, which contains many of the neurons that produce dopamine. Dopamine is a neurotransmitter in the brain responsible for sending signals between all of your cells, and it is classified as an excitatory neurotransmitter. In particular, dopamine is extremely involved in emotions like happiness, as well as movement. This is why in Parkinson's disease, loss of motor control and depression are seen overtime, as dopamine levels in the brain gradually decrease over the course of the disease. The protein aggregates are composed of a protein known as a-synuclein; these protein aggregates cause damage to the cells and eventually lead to their death, precipitating the loss of dopamine. One treatment used to manage Parkinson's symptoms is L-DOPA, a precursor to dopamine that can enter the brain through the bloodstream and then gets converted into dopamine. This external dopamine can help to slow down symptom progression to a point, but there comes a point where it is no longer effective. Deep-brain stimulation is also a promising treatment. With this procedure, physicians implant thin metal wires into the brain, which deliver targeted electrical pulses. While the mechanism is not entirely understood, DBS is thought to help normalize electrical currents in the brain. The net result of this is reduced tremors and motor symptoms for Parkinson's patients, allowing them to exist and navigate life much more easily.

Michael J Fox is a very famous actor, as seen in the video above, who has struggled with Parkinson's disease. As a result, he has donated lots of money and fundraised many research efforts to develop better tests and potential cures for the disease. Robin Williams also had a similar disease, Lewy Body dementia, which caused him to have Parkinson's-like symptoms (but it also caused greater damage throughout his brain). Ultimately, researchers are making great progress, and there is a lot of hope for a cure for Parkinson's.
