More on your brain!

The brain is composed of a variety of cells, the most important of these being neurons. Neurons are responsible for sending signals that allow us to move, sense, and think. The cell body, or soma, of these cells has two types of cytoplasmic extensions that enable it to transmit electrical signals across the body: dendrites that receive information and axons that transmit information through action potentials and release of neurotransmitters. Damage to these cytoplasmic projections disrupts this communication, for example, injury of the spinal cord results in loss of movement and sensation below the injury site. Therefore, it is very important to keep neurons safe and healthy for proper neurological functioning!

In zooming out, the neurons are the main communicators, but they fall into different categories depending on the region of the brain they occupy. There are 4 primary lobes of the brain, as well as two cortexes and the cerebellum. The lobes are the frontal lobes, the parietal lobes, the temporal lobes, and the occipital lobes. The cortexes are the motor and somatosensory cortex. The cerebellum is responsible for maintaining many autonomic (or automatic) functions of the body, such as breathing and balance! The motor cortex is what coordinates the movement of muscles throughout the body, and the somatosensory cortex allows for the processing of sensations like smell and touch. The various lobes also serve different functions; the frontal lobe is the executive function or "planning" lobe of the brain, the parietal lobes support sensory details from eating and reading, the occipital lobes process visual information, and the temporal lobes help with auditory processing and memories! Another important system is the limbic system, which is the center for emotion and memory processing in the brain and contains the amygdala, hippocampus, and the basal ganglia.

In the diseases discussed through this page, neuronal death is one of the key hallmarks of all of them. In each case, a different part of the brain is affected, as seen with the interactive map, but in all cases the damage done to the cells is irreversible, leading to their death and the overall degeneration seen with these diseases. It is important to note that the specific region of the brain impacted directly affects the symptoms of the patient, based on what general functions that region of the brain perform. This degeneration in the brain is usually caused by molecular pathways going awry! Usually, proteins have set functions in your neurons, but in these diseases, those proteins lose their normal function and sometimes gain functions that are harmful for the cell. Please feel free to explore each of the diseases above to learn which proteins are specifically implicated in each.
